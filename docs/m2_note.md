### Global Liquidity Weighting

The $104T nominal global M2 composite overstates some blocs’ contribution to globally active liquidity and understates others. Not all M2 is equal — capital account openness, reserve currency status, and the eurodollar system determine how much of each bloc’s money supply actually circulates in global markets.

| Bloc       | Nominal M2 | Est. Globally Active | Leakage | Key Mechanism |
|------------|------------|----------------------|---------|---------------|
| **US**     | $22.7T     | ~$19–20T             | ~85%    | Reserve currency. Settles ~60% of global trade and ~88% of FX transactions. $9T+ in USD credit extended to non-bank borrowers outside the US (BIS, Q2 2025). |
| **Eurozone** | $18.7T   | ~$8–9T               | ~45%    | Second reserve currency (~20% of global reserves). Open capital account. Euro cross-border credit growing 13% YoY (BIS, Q2 2025). |
| **China**  | $50.3T     | ~$3–5T               | ~8%     | Largest nominal M2 but mostly captive. Closed capital account, $50K individual FX quota. Leakage via yuan trade settlement (~3–4% of global payments), Hong Kong conduit, PBOC gold purchases, and Belt & Road lending. Yuan holds only ~2.3% of global reserves. |
| **Japan**  | $8.0T      | ~$2.5–3T             | ~35%    | Third reserve currency (~5.5% of reserves). Yen carry trade is a major global liquidity channel. Open capital account but domestic M2 largely trapped in JGBs by deflationary psychology. |
| **UK**     | $4.3T      | ~$2.5–3T             | ~65%    | Fourth reserve currency (~4.8% of reserves). London is the world’s largest FX trading centre. Punches far above its weight in global liquidity. |
| **Total**  | **$104T**  | **~$35–40T**         | **~36%**| |

**Two implications for the thesis:**

- US M2 changes are disproportionately powerful for BTC and risk assets. The US accounts for roughly half of all globally active liquidity despite being only ~22% of nominal M2. A $1T move in US M2 therefore moves global markets more than a $5T move in Chinese M2.

- De-dollarisation does not merely shift existing liquidity — it unlocks previously captive pools. If China’s capital account gradually opens and its leakage rises from ~8% to even 15–20%, that alone would add $3–6T of new globally active liquidity, independent of any central bank’s printing rate. This structural expansion of the investable pool flows directly into hard assets such as BTC and gold.
